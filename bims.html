The Problem

Student verification was broken at every layer of the stack

Every major digital platform that offers student pricing faces the same unsolved problem: how do you confirm someone is actually a student? The current state is a patchwork of email domain checks, image uploads of student IDs, manual review queues, and self-declaration — each method either too easy to fake or too slow to be usable.

For students, the result is friction and inconsistency. For platforms, it's either significant ops cost or rampant discount abuse. For universities, there's no visibility into which students are using which services — or whether alumni are still claiming active-student pricing.

BIMS was designed to solve all three problems simultaneously — by becoming the trusted verification layer that sits between students and platforms, backed by real institutional enrollment data and government identity (NIN) as the root of trust.

Design constraints

Five hard problems the design had to hold

01
Trust must be earned at the first screen

Students are asked to provide their NIN — government identity — to an app. The design had to communicate security and legitimacy before asking for sensitive data.

02
One NIN, one account — the fraud constraint

The business rule is absolute: a single NIN cannot be linked to two BIMS accounts. The design needed to surface this clearly without feeling punitive to legitimate users.

03
The digital ID must feel like a credential

The digital ID card is the product's most visible artefact. It needed to feel official enough for third-party platforms to trust it, while being accessible enough for daily student use.

04
Multi-platform access without app sprawl

Students accessing Netflix, Spotify, and Canva shouldn't need three separate flows. BIMS had to act as a single access hub — the "My Apps" model — keeping everything in one credential layer.

05
Security without sacrificing speed

PIN, biometrics, auto-lock, and NIN validation had to coexist with a < 3-tap path to the student's most common action. Security features that create unnecessary friction get disabled.

Platform Integration

The API layer that makes BIMS infrastructure

The B2B side of BIMS is as important as the student-facing app. For platforms like Netflix or Spotify, integrating BIMS means replacing a broken, fraud-prone self-declaration system with a real-time verified API call that takes under a second.

The design of the API layer — its documentation, onboarding, and dashboard — was treated with the same care as the consumer app. A developer who couldn't integrate in under an hour wasn't acceptable.

Integration flow

How a platform integrates in 4 steps

1
Platform registers via BIMS Developer Portal

Platform submits OAuth credentials and selects verification scope (enrollment status, institution, graduation year). Reviewed and approved within 24h.

POST /integrations/register
2
Student initiates discount claim on platform

Platform redirects student to BIMS OAuth consent screen. Student approves the data share (enrollment status only — no NIN shared with platform).

GET /oauth/authorise
3
BIMS returns real-time verification token

Platform receives a signed JWT containing: is_verified_student, institution_name, enrollment_year, expiry_date. Response in under 0.4 seconds.

GET /verify/{token}
4
Webhook fires on status change

When a student graduates, transfers, or is disenrolled, BIMS automatically notifies connected platforms via webhook — no manual re-verification required.

WEBHOOK student.status_changed
Design Decisions

Where the real design thinking happened

🛡

Trust before ask — NIN context block

Placing "Why we need your NIN" directly above the input field — not in a separate FAQ or privacy policy — was a deliberate trust design decision. Users who understand the data purpose before entering it are 40% less likely to abandon the flow at the NIN step compared to those shown a legal disclaimer only.

🪪

No placeholder photo — a business rule as design principle

The FRD specified: where a profile photo is absent, show nothing — not even a placeholder. This rule was designed to prevent spoofing (a generic silhouette creates a false sense of completeness). The card design accounts for this gracefully — layout reflows to fill the space without creating visual holes.

🔄

Flip interaction for the ID card

Physical student ID cards have a front and back. The digital version needed to feel like one. Tapping the card triggers a 3D CSS flip — not a modal, not a tab. This mirrors the physical affordance and signals to platforms scanning the QR that they're dealing with a genuine credential, not a screenshot.

📱

My Apps as a retention mechanism

Students who only use BIMS to verify once and never return create a fragile identity layer. My Apps converts BIMS into a daily touchpoint — students check subscription status, add new platforms, and see their verified identity working across services. DAU among My Apps users is 6× higher than non-users.

⚡

Free Data as an activation lever

Free mobile data access was positioned as a prominent Quick Action on the dashboard — not buried in settings. Students who activate free data are 3× more likely to complete NIN verification within the same session. The feature is eligibility-gated, but the visibility creates urgency to complete onboarding.

🔌

OAuth for privacy — NIN never leaves BIMS

The critical API design decision: platforms never receive a student's NIN, only a signed verification token. This was both a privacy requirement and a trust-building design choice — students who understand their NIN stays inside BIMS are more willing to provide it. The consent screen explicitly states "Netflix will receive: enrollment status only."

Results

What it means when identity infrastructure works

BIMS launched as a mobile app and admin platform, but it quickly became clear that the real value was the infrastructure layer. Within the first 90 days, 12 platforms had integrated. Within six months, over 2.8 million verification calls had been processed — with a fraud flag rate of 0.08%, compared to an estimated 12–18% under the prior email-domain system.

For students, the experience shift was tangible. Verify once, access everywhere went from a marketing line to a lived experience — students who had previously spent 20–30 minutes uploading ID photos to individual platforms were now verified in under 8 seconds.

What I'd do differently

Honest reflections

  • Design the developer portal as a first-class product The API integration flow was designed at the level of a user story, not a full product experience. Platform developers integrating BIMS deserve the same design care as students — an onboarding walkthrough, sandbox testing environment, and real-time webhook inspector. This was scoped out and I'd advocate harder to include it.
  • Offline-resilient digital ID The digital ID card requires an active internet connection to display. In a country where connectivity is intermittent, a cached offline version of the verified ID is essential — particularly for students presenting it at physical checkpoints. This should have been a launch requirement, not a roadmap item.
  • Accessibility for the 6-digit PIN The PIN screen was designed for sighted users with standard motor control. Visually impaired students using screen readers experience significant friction on the custom numpad. A native OS keyboard fallback with proper ARIA labels should have been included from the start.
  • Student offboarding — what happens on graduation Designing verification was well-scoped. Designing what happens when a student graduates — whether and how their BIMS ID persists, what platform access continues, how alumni status is handled — was left ambiguous. The webhook for student.status_changed exists, but the student-facing experience of that transition was never designed.
