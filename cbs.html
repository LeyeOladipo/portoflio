The Problem

School billing was broken for everyone involved

Nigerian schools collect fees through a fragmented ecosystem: bank transfers, POS machines at school gates, WhatsApp reminders from bursars, and handwritten receipts. For parents with more than one child — across different schools, different terms, different fee structures — keeping track is a part-time job.

For school administrators, the picture was no better. Reconciling payments manually, chasing outstanding balances, and generating reports for management meant that the finance team was perpetually behind on data that should be real-time.

CBS was conceived to fix both sides simultaneously — a consumer product that makes paying feel instant, and an admin platform that makes oversight feel effortless. The design challenge was building one coherent system that serves two fundamentally different mental models without compromising either.

What we were solving against

Six friction points across the payment journey

  • 01
    No unified parent view

    Parents with multiple wards at different schools had no single place to see all outstanding bills and payment history.

  • 02
    High friction payment flow

    Every payment required finding the school's account number, initiating a bank transfer, uploading proof, and waiting for manual confirmation — often days.

  • 03
    Wallet adoption cold-start problem

    KYC-gated wallet activation meant users abandoned before seeing the value. The gate had to feel like a door, not a wall.

  • 04
    Admin reconciliation delay

    School finance teams were reconciling payments 2–3 days after the fact. Outstanding fee reports were always stale.

  • 05
    No real-time visibility for parents

    Parents couldn't verify payment success without calling the school bursar. Trust in the system was low, leading to repeat payments and disputes.

  • 06
    School-switching for multi-school families

    Families with children at different institutions had no way to manage them within a single session — each school required a separate login.

Mobile App

Designed for the parent who's always in a rush

Every screen in the mobile app was designed around a single constraint: a parent should be able to pay any bill, for any child, in under three taps from the home screen.

Screen 01
Welcome — Trust from frame one

The onboarding screen had one job: establish that this is a real, secure product before asking for anything. The CBS logo mark with its layered rings signals technology and trust. The tagline "One app. All wards. Every school." tells parents immediately what the product does for them — not what it is.

Screen 02
Ward Dashboard — Full context, one tap

The ward dashboard was the most complex screen to design. A parent needs to see their child's school, current term, outstanding bills, and payment history without scrolling past the fold. The ward switcher at the top means switching children never requires leaving the context — it's a pivot, not a navigation.

Screen 03
KYC — Progressive disclosure of friction

KYC was the biggest drop-off risk. The decision: make required fields (name, phone, email) visually prominent and fast to fill, and collapse optional fields (NIN/BVN, address, DOB) under an expansion. The escape hatch — "You can pay bills without completing this" — reduced abandonment by removing the feeling of being forced.

Screen 04
Wallet Dashboard — Transparency as retention

The wallet dashboard surfaces transaction history as the primary content after balance. The insight: parents who can see every deduction — by ward, by bill type, by date — trust the wallet. Trust drives continued use. The green header gradient visually distinguishes the wallet section from the rest of the app.

Screen 05
Fund Wallet — Removing the single biggest barrier

Research showed that the funding step was where most users stalled. The quick-amount chips (₦5k–₦50k) removed the blank-field anxiety. Defaulting to Bank Transfer (zero fees) reduced cost objections. The payment method icons were validated against what parents actually use — OPay and PalmPay ranked ahead of card in user testing.

Screen 06
Success State — The most underdesigned screen

Payment success screens are often afterthoughts. Here, it's a trust-building moment: receipt data is surfaced immediately, no hunting required. The contextual nudge ("Grace's levy is due in 5 days") uses the post-payment moment — when the wallet is freshly funded — to drive the next payment without feeling pushy. Conversion from success to second payment was 28% in the first month.

Admin Platform

The school's financial command centre

While parents use the mobile app to pay, school finance teams needed a web platform with a completely different mandate: real-time reconciliation, student-level visibility, and management reporting — without requiring data exports or phone calls.

Real-time over reconciliation

The admin dashboard was designed around eliminating the bursar's daily reconciliation ritual. Every wallet payment appears instantly with a Confirmed status — no manual matching required. The "Verifying" state handles bank transfers in progress transparently, so the team knows the payment is in flight.

Class-level intervention signals

The collection-by-class bar chart turns a management report into an operational tool. When JS1 hits 50%, the head of finance can instruct form teachers to follow up — without running a separate query. The amber and red colouring at 60% and below isn't decorative; it's a decision trigger.

Design Decisions

Where the real design thinking happened

🚪

KYC as a door, not a wall

Wallet activation requires KYC — but bill payment doesn't. The design makes this distinction visible at every friction point: the KYC form has a clear escape route ("You can pay bills without this"), the wallet prompt has a "Maybe Later" option, and the app never blocks users from their primary job. This reduced KYC abandonment from an estimated 60% to 31%.

👥

Ward switching as a pivot, not a flow

Early designs treated multi-ward management as a separate section. User testing showed parents think in terms of "who am I paying for right now" — not "which child's settings should I open." Redesigning ward switching as a persistent dropdown at the top of the dashboard reduced navigation depth for multi-child families by 3 taps per session.

💡

Contextual wallet promotion, not pop-ups

The strategy for wallet adoption was to place prompts at moments of genuine value — payment completion, bill due reminder, insufficient balance. Banner pop-ups were never used. Each prompt showed a specific benefit relevant to the current context. This approach achieved 67% activation against a 40% launch target.

✅

The success screen as a conversion engine

Post-payment success screens are consistently underdesigned. CBS uses this moment to show a "next bill due" notification — surfacing Grace's levy when Daniel's fees have just been paid — while the wallet is funded and the user is in payment mode. This single screen change drove a 28% same-session second payment rate.

⚡

Quick-fund chips eliminate blank-field anxiety

The Fund Wallet screen originally had a single number input. Testing showed parents pausing to calculate how much they needed. Quick-amount chips (₦5k, ₦10k, ₦25k, ₦50k) covering the most common funding patterns removed the calculation step entirely. Average fund session time dropped from 48 seconds to 14 seconds.

📊

Admin colour semantics as operational logic

In the admin dashboard, colour is functional — not decorative. Teal means the school's target is on track. Amber means intervention is warranted. Red means immediate action is needed. This three-state semantic system is consistent across every chart, badge, and status indicator on the platform, reducing the time-to-insight for bursars from minutes to seconds.

Results

What shipped and what it changed

CBS launched on iOS and Android alongside the web admin platform. Within the first 60 days, the product had materially changed how parents interacted with school billing and how schools tracked their income.

The wallet activation rate exceeded target by 27 percentage points. This was largely driven by the contextual promotion strategy — presenting wallet activation at moments of genuine user value rather than as a gate at onboarding.

For school administrators, the shift from manual reconciliation to real-time confirmation reduced the finance team's workload by an estimated two hours per day during peak payment periods. The class-level collection chart became the most-used feature in the admin dashboard.

What I'd do differently

Honest reflections on the engagement

  • Offline-first for the mobile app A significant portion of users are in areas with intermittent connectivity. Designing around an always-online assumption created frustration during sessions that started on WiFi and dropped to mobile data. A future version would handle partial connectivity states — showing cached bill data and queuing payment attempts — far more gracefully.
  • Student-facing bill view was an afterthought The FRD defined students as secondary users who could "view billing information." In practice, many boarding school students use the app independently. A student-specific view — simplified, read-only, with notification preferences — would have meaningfully improved adoption in that segment.
  • Admin onboarding for school setup The admin platform assumed schools had already been configured. New school onboarding — setting up classes, defining bill structures, linking student records — was handled by a separate ops team. A self-serve school onboarding wizard would have dramatically reduced implementation time.
  • Earlier A/B testing on the KYC escape hatch The "Maybe Later" button placement and copy was decided by intuition. Post-launch data suggested that moving the escape hatch to directly below the primary CTA (rather than below the form) would have further reduced abandonment. Earlier multivariate testing would have surfaced this faster.
