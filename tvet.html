The Problem

A disbursement process with no structure, no trail, no trust

Nigeria's TVET programme funds secondary school students with recurring stipends — a direct intervention designed to reduce dropout rates by absorbing the cost of attending school. The intent was clear. The execution wasn't.

Before this platform existed, disbursement was handled through a patchwork of spreadsheets, bank intermediaries, and informal sign-off chains. Money moved without accountability. Students couldn't verify they'd been paid. Parents had no visibility. Administrators couldn't prove approvals had been obtained before funds were released.

The federal oversight body needed a system that could impose structure on a genuinely complex financial process — across multiple states, multiple schools, and thousands of student records — without requiring the users operating it to be technically sophisticated.

What we inherited

Core constraints the design had to solve

01
No single source of truth

Student enrollment data lived simultaneously in spreadsheets, Krystal's system, and school admin files — often contradicting each other at the point of disbursement.

02
Zero audit trail on disbursements

Funds could be approved verbally and released without documented sign-off at any level. There was no system of record.

03
Role confusion at every tier

TVET federal admins, school admins, and state officers had overlapping responsibilities with no defined system boundary or permission model.

04
Students entirely in the dark

Recipients had no way to verify whether a disbursement had been initiated, approved, or credited to their account.

05
Manual, disconnected wallet creation

Zenith Bank wallets were opened ad-hoc with no structured link between enrollment data and banking infrastructure.

06
No resubmission flow for rejections

Rejected disbursements fell into a void — initiators weren't notified of reasons or given a formal path to correct and resubmit.

Screen 01

Super Admin — System Dashboard

The command centre. Designed for the federal oversight team to monitor system-wide health — wallet creation status, disbursement pipeline, and cross-school performance — without drowning in granular data.

What it does

Gives the Super Admin a real-time read on system health — wallet creation rate, disbursement pipeline, school-level compliance — without clicking into individual records. The design surfaces anomalies (low wallet activation, missed disbursements) as the primary signal.

Design decision

Avoided aggregation-only views. The table at the bottom keeps individual schools visible, so a national-level admin can spot a single school in distress without running a report. Status chips use a deliberate four-state system: Active, Sync Pending, Action Needed, and Approved — not boolean.

Screen 02

Initiator — Create Disbursement

The most sensitive interaction in the system. A TVET Initiator configures a disbursement — selects schools, sets amounts, schedules the batch — and submits it to the Reviewer queue. Every decision is logged.

Initiator form — structured input, not free-form

Every field maps to a downstream consequence: scope affects recipient count, reference IDs feed the audit trail, document uploads satisfy reviewer requirements. The form was designed to prevent common errors before submission — not surface them afterward.

Reviewer queue — contextual, not tabular

Reviewers see disbursements as decision items — not spreadsheet rows. Urgent flags, missing document alerts, and one-click action panels were designed so a reviewer can assess and act on a request in under 90 seconds without leaving the queue.

Screen 03 & 04

Student Dashboard & Final Approver View

Two ends of the same system. The student sees their wallet balance and disbursement history — simple, transparent, trusted. The Approver sees the full workflow audit trail before authorising fund release.

Student view — radical transparency

Students had zero visibility under the previous system. The design prioritises one thing above all: confirming that money has arrived, and showing exactly why. NIN verification status, wallet ID, and a downloadable statement give students the paper trail they previously had to chase.

Approver view — full context before action

A final approver who clicks "Authorise" is releasing tens of millions of naira. The design ensures they see the complete trail — initiator, reviewer comment, amounts, recipients — before the action button is reachable. The irreversibility warning is not opt-out.

Design Process

How we got from chaos to structure

01
Stakeholder mapping & role discovery

Before drawing a single screen, I mapped every human in the system — TVET federal staff, Krystal data teams, school admins, Zenith Bank, SwiftLink, students, and parents — and documented what each needed to do, see, and trust the platform to handle correctly.

02
Workflow architecture & data model review

I worked with the product lead to trace every data flow: enrollment sync from Krystal API, wallet creation via Zenith, disbursement trigger via SwiftLink. Designing the UI required understanding what state each entity was in at every step — not just what it looked like.

03
Role-specific journey mapping

Six user roles, each with a distinct mental model and task context. I mapped separate critical paths for each — Super Admin's oversight loop, the three-tier Initiator-Reviewer-Approver chain, School Admin's sync workflow, and the Student's verification and visibility needs.

04
Iterative prototyping & stakeholder review

High-fidelity prototypes were reviewed with TVET programme officers before any development began. Key changes emerged from these sessions: the irreversibility warning on final approval, the mandatory comment field for all decisions, and the resubmission flow for rejected disbursements.

Role System

Six roles. One coherent system.

System-wide

Super Admin

Full access · All schools · All data

  • Manage all user accounts and role assignments
  • View system-wide analytics and audit logs
  • Monitor wallet creation and disbursement health
  • Generate cross-school reports
School-scoped

School Admin

Single institution · Read + sync

  • View students enrolled in their school
  • Initiate API sync with Krystal
  • Monitor wallet status for their cohort
  • View school-level disbursement history
Level 1

TVET Initiator

Disbursement workflow · Create only

  • Create and configure disbursement requests
  • Select school targets and set amounts
  • Attach supporting documentation
  • Receive rejections and resubmit
Level 2

TVET Reviewer

Disbursement workflow · Review only

  • Review Initiator submissions with full context
  • Verify student eligibility and data accuracy
  • Approve, reject, or request documentation
  • All decisions attributed and timestamped
Level 3

TVET Approver

Final authority · Authorise execution

  • Final sign-off on reviewed disbursements
  • Sees complete workflow trail before acting
  • Authorises SwiftLink fund release
  • Sets disbursement schedules and monitors success
Recipient

Student & Parent

Personal data only · Read + verify

  • Provide NIN for wallet verification (student)
  • View wallet balance and transaction history
  • Download invoices and disbursement receipts
  • Receive notifications on all credits
Key Design Decisions

Where the real design work happened

⛔

Mandatory comments on every approval decision

Approvers and Reviewers cannot submit an action without a written comment. This wasn't requested in the PRD — it emerged from observing how verbal approvals had been abused in the prior system. The UI enforces accountability at the point of action, not through after-the-fact reporting.

🔄

Resubmission flow for rejected disbursements

Under the previous process, a rejected disbursement had no formal path back. I designed a rejection → reason → edit → resubmit loop that keeps the workflow moving and forces Reviewers to document their rejection rationale — creating a learning loop for Initiators and an evidence chain for auditors.

👁

Student-facing NIN verification as trust signal

Students providing their NIN wasn't just a data requirement — it was a trust-building moment. I designed the verification flow to surface progress clearly ("NIN submitted → Under review → Verified"), reducing support requests and giving students a sense of agency in their own onboarding.

⚠️

Irreversibility warning on final authorisation

Before the Approver can act, a persistent warning communicates exactly what happens next: funds are released to thousands of wallets, immediately, and cannot be recalled. This is a friction-by-design decision — the brief pause it creates is the point. High-stakes action requires deliberate confirmation.

🔗

Four-state wallet status model

Rather than a binary active/inactive wallet view, I designed a four-state system: Active, Pending, Sync Failed, and Action Needed. This gave school admins and the Super Admin a precise operational picture — not just whether a wallet exists, but what needs to happen next to make it function.

📋

Role-scoped dashboards, not one dashboard filtered

Each role gets a dashboard built for their job, not a single view with permissions applied. A Reviewer's home screen is a prioritised queue. An Approver's is a decision stage. A Student's is a wallet. These are different mental models — they deserved different interfaces.

Results & Reflection

What shipping this system actually meant

The TVET Portal replaced a process that had failed students for years. Disbursements that previously took weeks to confirm — and were sometimes disputed entirely — now have a documented, timestamped chain from initiation to wallet credit.

10,847 students across Nigeria now have Zenith Bank wallets tied to their verified identity. They can see their balance. They can download a receipt. They can show a parent that money arrived. That's not a small thing.

For TVET's federal office, the three-tier approval workflow eliminated the informal sign-off culture that had created audit vulnerabilities. Every naira disbursed now has a documented decision trail with named approvers.

"We needed a system that could hold up to scrutiny — from auditors, from state governments, and from the students themselves. What was delivered gave us that."

— Programme Director, TVET Implementation Office
What I'd do differently

Honest reflections from the engagement

  • Earlier usability testing with school admins The portal was built primarily with TVET federal staff input. School admins — who do the most repetitive, high-frequency work in the system — were consulted late. Their sync workflows needed significant adjustment post-launch.
  • Mobile-first for students from day one Students access the portal primarily on mobile. The responsive design was added as a consideration, not a constraint. In a future version, the student experience would be designed mobile-native, not desktop-adapted.
  • Designing for failure states earlier The Zenith Bank API and SwiftLink integration had failure modes that weren't fully surfaced during design. Error states — failed wallet creation, timed-out disbursements — were retrofitted rather than designed proactively.
  • A parent-specific onboarding flow Parents/guardians were treated as a secondary persona throughout. Given that many guardians are the primary financial decision-makers in these households, a dedicated parent onboarding and verification flow would have made the platform significantly more trusted at the household level.
